## 1. Learning Objectives

After this lesson you will be able to:

- Explain **why we replicate** (availability, read scaling, latency/locality, durability) and the fundamental cost it introduces: **keeping copies consistent** (the subject of all of Part 10).
- Describe the three replication topologies — **leader-follower (single-leader)**, **multi-leader**, and **leaderless** — with their write paths, tradeoffs, and where each fits.
- Reason about the **defining tension**: single-leader avoids write conflicts (all writes ordered by one leader) but limits write scaling/availability; multi-leader and leaderless allow writes anywhere (better availability/locality) but introduce **write conflicts** that must be resolved (10.4).
- Map topologies to real systems and use cases (single-leader RDBMS/most systems; multi-leader multi-datacenter/offline; leaderless Dynamo-style).

## 2. Motivation — Copies everywhere, and the price of keeping them honest

**Replication** — keeping copies of the same data on multiple nodes — is unavoidable at scale, and we've met it repeatedly (read replicas — 5.4.2; replicated shards — 7.3; ISR in the log — 9.3; state-machine replication via consensus — 8.3.3). We replicate for four reasons: **availability** (survive node/datacenter failure — a copy takes over), **read scaling** (spread reads across copies — 7.5), **latency/locality** (serve users from a nearby copy — Part 13), and **durability** (data survives on multiple machines). These are compelling, which is why virtually every serious system replicates.

But replication's benefit — many copies — is also its central problem: **how do you keep the copies consistent when writes happen?** If a write updates one copy, the others are now **stale** until the write propagates; if writes can happen on **multiple** copies concurrently, they can **conflict**. This is the fountainhead of *all* of Part 10 — consistency models (10.5), CAP/PACELC (10.7/10.8), conflict resolution (10.4), and the replication-lag anomalies (10.3) all stem from the tension between "have copies" and "keep them consistent." The **topology** — *how* writes and updates flow among the copies — is the first and most consequential replication decision, because it determines the entire consistency/availability/conflict landscape. **Single-leader** funnels all writes through one node (simple, no conflicts, but the leader limits write scaling and is a failover point); **multi-leader** allows writes at several leaders (great for multi-datacenter/offline, but writes **conflict** and must be resolved); **leaderless** lets any replica accept writes with quorums (high availability, but weaker consistency and conflict handling). This lesson lays out the three topologies and their tradeoffs — the frame for everything else in Part 10.

## 3. Theory — From first principles

### 3.1 Why replicate, and the consistency cost

`[CS]` Replication keeps **copies of the same data on multiple nodes**, for:
- **Availability / fault tolerance** — a copy survives node/rack/DC failure (failover — 5.4.2, Part 11); no single point of data loss.
- **Read scaling** — spread reads across replicas (7.5) — the cheap way to scale reads.
- **Latency / locality** — serve users from a **nearby** replica (geo-distribution — Part 13) → lower latency.
- **Durability** — data on N machines survives failures better than on one.

**The cost:** the moment data is **written**, copies must be **kept consistent**. Between a write and its propagation, replicas **diverge** (staleness → **replication lag** — 10.2/10.3); if writes happen on **multiple** replicas, they can **conflict** (10.4). Replication is **easy for reads, hard for writes** (7.5) — and *how hard* depends on the **topology** (§3.2–3.4). All of Part 10 is about managing this consistency cost.

### 3.2 Leader-follower (single-leader / primary-replica) replication

`[CS]` **One replica is the leader (primary); the others are followers (replicas).**
- **Write path:** **all writes go to the leader**, which applies them and then **propagates** the change (via a **replication log** — the WAL/change stream, 5.3.1/9.8) to followers, which apply them **in the same order**. 
- **Read path:** reads can go to the **leader** (fresh) or **followers** (scale reads, but possibly stale — replication lag — 10.2/10.3). This is **read/write splitting** (7.5).
- **Key property — no write conflicts:** because **one leader orders all writes**, there's a **single, definitive order** of writes (a total order — 8.2.3) → **no conflicts** to resolve. This is the huge simplification of single-leader.
- **Costs:** the **leader is a write bottleneck** (all writes funnel through it — write scaling needs **sharding**, 7.3, giving one leader *per shard*); the leader is a **failover point** (if it dies, elect a new leader — 8.3.5 — a brief unavailability + possible data loss on async replication — 10.2); a partition can strand the leader (CAP — 10.7).
- **The default and most common topology** — relational databases (Postgres/MySQL primary-replica), MongoDB replica sets, Kafka partition leaders (9.3), most systems. Simple, strongly-orderable, well-understood.

### 3.3 Multi-leader (multi-master) replication

`[CS]` **Multiple replicas act as leaders**, each accepting writes, and they **replicate to each other**.
- **Write path:** a client writes to **any** leader (typically the nearest); each leader applies locally and **asynchronously replicates** to the other leaders, which apply the change.
- **Why:** primarily for **multi-datacenter** (a leader **per datacenter** → local low-latency writes in each region, and each DC keeps working during inter-DC network issues — 10.7/Part 13) and **offline/collaborative** clients (each device is a "leader" accepting local writes, syncing when reconnected — e.g., calendar apps, collaborative docs).
- **The defining problem — write conflicts:** two leaders can **concurrently modify the same data** (before they've synced) → **conflict** (which value wins? — 8.2.2 concurrency). Multi-leader **must detect and resolve conflicts** (10.4 — LWW, CRDTs, version vectors, application merge). This is the core cost and complexity.
- **Tradeoffs:** **better write availability/locality** (write locally in each region, survive inter-region partitions) but **conflict resolution complexity** and **weaker consistency** (concurrent writes → eventual convergence — 10.5). Use when the multi-DC/offline benefit justifies the conflict cost.
- Examples: multi-DC RDBMS setups, CouchDB, collaborative/offline apps, some Cassandra multi-DC configs. *(Representative.)*

### 3.4 Leaderless replication

`[CS]` **No leader — any replica accepts writes directly**, and consistency is achieved via **quorums** (8.3.4) rather than a leader ordering writes. (The Dynamo model — 8.3.4/8.2.2.)
- **Write path:** the client (or a coordinator) sends the write to **several replicas** (N replicas); the write succeeds when **W** acknowledge (8.3.4). No single leader.
- **Read path:** the client reads from **R** replicas and reconciles (takes the newest via version — 8.2.2), with **R + W > N** ensuring a read sees the latest write (8.3.4).
- **Consistency mechanisms:** **quorums (R+W>N)**, **read repair** (fix stale replicas found during a read — 8.3.4), **anti-entropy** (background sync of replicas), and **version vectors** (detect concurrent writes → conflicts — 8.2.2/10.4).
- **Tradeoffs:** **high availability** (no leader to fail; write to any W reachable replicas — **sloppy quorums** even during partitions — 8.3.4/10.9) and **tunable consistency** (R/W dials — 7.5/8.3.4), but **weaker/eventual consistency** by default, **conflicts** from concurrent writes (10.4), and the subtleties that **quorums ≠ linearizability** (8.3.4 §3.6). 
- Examples: Amazon Dynamo, Cassandra, Riak, Voldemort. *(Representative.)* Favors **availability** (AP — 10.7) — the classic "always writable, eventually consistent" store.

### 3.5 The unifying tension: single-orderer vs write-anywhere

`[CS]` The three topologies are points on one spectrum defined by **where writes are ordered**:
- **Single-leader:** **one orderer** → a single total order of writes → **no conflicts**, but the leader **limits write scale/availability** and is a **failover point**.
- **Multi-leader / leaderless:** **writes anywhere** → **no single order** → **better write availability/locality/scale**, but **concurrent writes conflict** and must be **detected + resolved** (10.4), yielding **weaker (eventual/causal) consistency** (10.5).
**The core tradeoff:** **avoiding conflicts (single-leader) vs write-anywhere availability (multi-leader/leaderless).** You can't have both — funneling writes through one orderer gives clean ordering but a bottleneck/single point; allowing writes anywhere gives availability/locality but conflicts. This tension **is** the CAP/PACELC choice (10.7/10.8) at the topology level, and it determines whether you'll need conflict resolution (10.4) and what consistency you can offer (10.5).

### 3.6 Choosing a topology

`[BP]`
| Need | Topology |
|---|---|
| Simplicity, strong ordering, no conflicts, typical app | **Single-leader** (the default) |
| Write scaling beyond one node | **Single-leader + sharding** (one leader per shard — 7.3) |
| Multi-datacenter low-latency local writes / survive inter-DC partition | **Multi-leader** (accept conflict resolution) |
| Offline/collaborative clients (each writes locally, syncs) | **Multi-leader** (or CRDTs — 10.4) |
| Maximum write availability, tunable consistency, "always writable" | **Leaderless** (Dynamo-style; accept eventual consistency + conflicts) |

**Default to single-leader** (simplest, no conflicts) — it serves the vast majority of systems; **shard** it for write scale. Reach for **multi-leader** only when multi-DC/offline demands local writes (and you'll pay conflict-resolution cost), and **leaderless** when you need Dynamo-style high availability with tunable consistency. The topology choice cascades into every other Part 10 decision.

## 5. Real-World Analogy

Imagine keeping a **shared document** in sync across a team.

- **Single-leader:** there's **one designated editor** — all changes must go **through them**. They apply edits **in order** and send updated copies to everyone else. There's **never a conflict** (one person decides the sequence), and everyone eventually has the same version. But that editor is a **bottleneck** (all changes queue behind them), and if they're **out sick**, nobody can make changes until you appoint a new editor (failover). This is how most teams sensibly work — one source of edits.
- **Multi-leader:** now **several editors** (one per office) can each make changes **locally** and mail their changes to the others. Great when offices are far apart (each works fast locally, and keeps working even if the mail between offices is cut). But if **two editors change the same paragraph** before syncing, you get a **conflict** — two different versions of that paragraph — and you need a rule to **reconcile** them (whose wins? merge both? — 10.4). This is exactly how **offline-capable collaborative apps** work (each device edits locally, syncs later, resolves conflicts).
- **Leaderless:** there's **no designated editor at all** — anyone can write to **several copies at once**, and to read you check **several copies and take the newest**. As long as you write to and read from **overlapping** sets of copies (quorums — R+W>N), you'll see recent changes. It's **extremely resilient** (no single editor to lose; you can always write to *some* copies), but copies can **temporarily disagree** (eventual consistency), and **concurrent writes conflict** and must be reconciled.
- **The core tradeoff:** **one editor = clean order but a bottleneck/single point; many editors (or none) = resilient and fast-local but conflicts to resolve.** You choose based on whether avoiding conflicts or maximizing write availability matters more.

## 6. Industry Example

- **Single-leader RDBMS / replica sets** `[CONV]`: Postgres/MySQL primary + read replicas, MongoDB replica sets, Kafka partition leaders (9.3) — the dominant topology; no write conflicts, read scaling via followers (5.4.2/7.5). *(Representative.)*
- **Multi-leader multi-datacenter** `[CONV]`: multi-region deployments with a write leader per region for local-latency writes and inter-region resilience, accepting conflict resolution (§3.3, Part 13). *(Representative.)*
- **Offline/collaborative apps** `[CONV]`: calendars, note apps, collaborative editors treat each device as a leader (local writes) syncing later — multi-leader with conflict resolution/CRDTs (10.4). *(Representative.)*
- **Dynamo/Cassandra/Riak (leaderless)** `[CONV]`: any replica accepts writes; quorums (R+W>N), read repair, anti-entropy, version vectors; high availability, tunable/eventual consistency (§3.4, 8.3.4/8.2.2). *(Representative.)*
- **Single-leader + sharding for write scale** `[BP]`: one leader per shard (Vitess/Citus, Kafka partitions) — combine no-conflicts single-leader with horizontal write scaling (7.3, §3.6). *(Representative.)*

## 7. Implementation Details — choosing and operating

- **Default to single-leader** — simplest, no conflicts, strong ordering; **shard** for write scale (one leader per shard — 7.3) (§3.2/3.6) `[BP]`.
- **Use multi-leader only when justified** — multi-DC local-latency writes or offline/collaborative clients — and **plan conflict resolution** (10.4) as a first-class concern (§3.3).
- **Use leaderless (Dynamo-style)** when you need maximum write availability + tunable consistency, accepting eventual consistency + conflicts + quorum subtleties (8.3.4/10.9) (§3.4).
- **Understand what your topology implies** for consistency (10.5), conflicts (10.4), lag anomalies (10.3), and CAP behavior (10.7) — the topology cascades into all of these.
- **Route reads deliberately** — leader/fresh vs follower/replica (stale, read scaling), handling read-your-writes (10.3, 7.5).
- **Plan failover** for single-leader (leader election + fencing — 8.3.5/8.3.6; possible data loss on async — 10.2) and understand multi-leader/leaderless survive leader loss more gracefully (no single leader).
- **Replicate each shard** (single-leader-per-shard + followers) for write scale + read scale + availability (7.3/7.5).

## 8. Advantages (by topology)

- **Single-leader:** no write conflicts (single order), simple, strong ordering, mature; read scaling via followers.
- **Multi-leader:** local low-latency writes per DC, survives inter-DC partitions, supports offline/collaborative writes.
- **Leaderless:** high write availability (no leader to fail; write to any W), tunable consistency (R/W), survives partitions (sloppy quorums — 10.9).
- **All:** availability, read scaling, latency/locality, durability (the reasons to replicate — §3.1).

---

## 9. Disadvantages (by topology)

- **Single-leader:** leader is a **write bottleneck** (scale via sharding) and a **failover point** (brief unavailability + possible data loss on async — 10.2); a partition can strand it (CAP — 10.7).
- **Multi-leader:** **write conflicts** (must detect + resolve — 10.4); weaker consistency; complexity.
- **Leaderless:** **eventual consistency** by default; **conflicts** (concurrent writes — 10.4); quorums ≠ linearizability (8.3.4 §3.6); operational subtlety (read repair, anti-entropy, sloppy quorums).
- **All:** the fundamental cost — **keeping copies consistent** (lag/staleness — 10.2/10.3; conflicts — 10.4).

---

## 10. When NOT to use each

- **Don't use multi-leader/leaderless** when single-leader suffices — you take on conflict resolution and weaker consistency for no benefit (§3.6) — most systems don't need them.
- **Don't use single-leader** for genuine multi-DC low-latency writes or offline clients — a single leader forces remote writes / can't work offline (use multi-leader) (§3.3).
- **Don't use leaderless** when you need strong consistency/ordering without conflict-handling complexity — single-leader is simpler (§3.4/3.6).
- **Don't rely on a single leader for write scale** — shard it (one leader per shard) (§3.2, 7.3).
- **Don't adopt multi-leader without a conflict-resolution strategy** — concurrent writes will corrupt/lose data (10.4).

---

## 11. Common Mistakes

1. **Multi-leader without conflict resolution** — concurrent writes conflict and silently lose/corrupt data (§3.3, 10.4).
2. **Expecting single-leader to scale writes** — it can't beyond one node; shard it (§3.2, 7.3).
3. **Assuming leaderless = strong consistency** — it's eventual by default; quorums ≠ linearizability (§3.4, 8.3.4).
4. **Reading from followers/replicas expecting freshness** — replication lag → stale reads (10.2/10.3).
5. **Ignoring failover behavior** — single-leader failover has unavailability + possible data loss (async — 10.2); no fencing → split brain (8.3.5/8.3.6).
6. **Choosing multi-leader/leaderless for fashion** — taking on conflict/consistency complexity without the multi-DC/availability need (§3.6).
7. **Not replicating shards** — sharding for write scale without replicas → no read scale / no HA per shard (7.3/7.5).

## 13. Production Pitfalls

- **Multi-leader conflict corruption:** concurrent writes to the same key in two regions conflict; without resolution, one silently overwrites the other (lost update) or state diverges (§3.3, 10.4).
- **Single-leader write bottleneck:** all writes funnel through one leader; write throughput hits a ceiling; the fix (sharding) wasn't planned (§3.2, 7.3).
- **Stale reads from replicas:** reading from followers/leaderless with lag → users see old data (read-your-writes violation — 10.3).
- **Failover data loss / split brain:** async single-leader failover loses un-replicated writes; no fencing → two leaders (10.2, 8.3.5/8.3.6).
- **Leaderless "strong consistency" surprise:** team assumed R+W>N gives linearizability; concurrent writes and edge cases show it doesn't (§3.4, 8.3.4).
- **Unplanned conflict resolution:** adopting multi-leader/leaderless without deciding LWW/CRDT/merge → data loss or divergence (10.4).

---

## 14. Optimization Techniques

- **Single-leader + sharding** — no conflicts + write scaling (one leader per shard) (§3.2/3.6, 7.3) `[BP]`.
- **Replicate each shard + read/write splitting** — write scale + read scale + HA (7.3/7.5, 5.4.2).
- **Multi-leader per DC** for local-latency writes + inter-DC resilience, with a chosen conflict-resolution strategy (§3.3, 10.4).
- **Leaderless with tuned R/W** for availability + tunable consistency (8.3.4/10.9); read repair + anti-entropy to converge (§3.4).
- **CRDTs** for multi-leader/leaderless data that can be made conflict-free (auto-merge — 10.4, 8.2.2).
- **Fenced leader failover** for single-leader HA (8.3.5/8.3.6, Part 11).
- **Locality-aware read routing** (nearby replica) for latency, handling staleness/read-your-writes (10.3, Part 13).

## 15. Summary

**Replication** — copies of the same data on multiple nodes — is done for **availability** (survive failures), **read scaling** (7.5), **latency/locality** (serve from nearby copies), and **durability**. Its fundamental cost is **keeping the copies consistent when writes happen** — the fountainhead of all of Part 10 (staleness/lag — 10.2/10.3, conflicts — 10.4, consistency models — 10.5, CAP/PACELC — 10.7/10.8). The **topology** — how writes and updates flow among copies — is the first and most consequential choice. **Single-leader (leader-follower)** funnels **all writes through one leader** that orders them and propagates to followers (via a replication log — 5.3.1/9.8); reads go to the leader (fresh) or followers (stale — read/write splitting, 7.5). Its huge advantage is **no write conflicts** (one orderer → a single total order — 8.2.3); its costs are the **leader as a write bottleneck** (scale via **sharding** — one leader per shard, 7.3) and a **failover point** (election + fencing — 8.3.5/8.3.6; possible data loss on async — 10.2). It's the **default and most common** topology. **Multi-leader** lets **several leaders each accept writes** and replicate to each other — used for **multi-datacenter** (local low-latency writes per region, inter-DC resilience) and **offline/collaborative** clients — but its defining problem is **write conflicts** (concurrent writes to the same data must be **detected and resolved** — 10.4), yielding weaker consistency and complexity. **Leaderless (Dynamo-style)** has **no leader — any replica accepts writes**, with consistency via **quorums (R+W>N)**, **read repair**, **anti-entropy**, and **version vectors** (8.3.4/8.2.2) — giving **high write availability** (write to any W reachable replicas, even during partitions via sloppy quorums — 10.9) and **tunable consistency**, but **eventual consistency** and **conflicts** by default (and quorums ≠ linearizability). The unifying tension: **single-orderer (single-leader) = clean ordering, no conflicts, but bottleneck/failover point** vs **write-anywhere (multi-leader/leaderless) = availability/locality/scale, but conflicts and weaker consistency** — which **is** the CAP/PACELC choice at the topology level (10.7/10.8). **Default to single-leader** (simplest, shard for write scale); use **multi-leader** for multi-DC/offline (paying conflict-resolution cost) and **leaderless** for Dynamo-style high availability with tunable consistency. The topology choice cascades into every other Part 10 decision.
