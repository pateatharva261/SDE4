## 2. Problem statement

Design a **news feed** (Twitter/X timeline, Facebook feed, Instagram home) at depth: when a user opens the app, show a **ranked, recent list of posts from accounts they follow**, updated as new posts arrive. This extends **19.1.5** — the crux is the **fan-out strategy** (how a post reaches millions of followers' feeds) and the **celebrity/hot-key** problem (7.4). It's the archetypal **massive-read-fan-out + write-amplification** design.

---

## 3. The design (framework — 1.3.1)

### 3.1 Requirements

`[BP]`
- **Functional:** post creation; a **home feed** = merged, ranked posts from followed accounts; **pagination** (infinite scroll); near-real-time updates.
- **Non-functional:** **read-heavy** (feed loads ≫ posts — the dominant path); **low latency** feed load (users wait); **eventual consistency** OK (a post appearing a few seconds late is fine — 10.5); highly available; huge scale (fan-out amplification).
- `[BP]` **Key signal:** it's about **getting posts to followers efficiently** — a **fan-out + read-scaling** problem where the **follower-graph skew** (celebrities — 7.4) dominates the design.

### 3.2 Estimation (1.1.4)

`[BP]` Illustrative: reads (feed loads) vastly outnumber writes (posts) — say **100:1+**. But a **single post by a celebrity with 100M followers** implies **100M feed insertions** if you push → **write amplification** is the killer (7.4). → The design revolves around **not doing 100M writes per celebrity post**.

### 3.3 API + data model

`[BP]`
- `POST /posts`, `GET /feed?cursor=…` (paginated), `POST /follow`.
- **Data:** `posts` (by postId, sharded — 7.3), `follows` (edges: follower→followee), and **precomputed feeds** (`userId → list of postIds`, cached — Part 6). Use **cursor-based pagination** (not offset) for stable infinite scroll over a changing list (§3.6).

### 3.4 The core: fan-out strategy (the crux — 19.1.5, deeper)

`[CS]` How a new post reaches followers' feeds `[BP]`:
- **Fan-out-on-write (push):** when you post, **immediately insert the postId into every follower's precomputed feed** (in cache/store). **Feed read = cheap** (just fetch your precomputed list — great for the read-heavy path). **Cost:** a post triggers **as many writes as you have followers** → **write amplification**; catastrophic for **celebrities** (100M writes per post — 7.4) and wasteful for **inactive followers**.
- **Fan-out-on-read (pull):** store only each user's own posts; when a follower opens their feed, **query all followees' recent posts and merge/rank at read time**. **Post = cheap** (one write); **read = expensive** (scatter-gather across many followees — 18.7-style — every feed load). Bad for the read-heavy path, but immune to the celebrity write problem.
- **Hybrid (the answer):** **push for normal users** (cheap, fast reads) but **pull for celebrities** — don't fan-out a celebrity's post to 100M feeds; instead, **at read time, merge in the (few) celebrities you follow** on top of your pushed feed. Combines cheap reads (mostly precomputed) with no celebrity write explosion (7.4). `[BP]` **This is the standard senior answer.**

### 3.5 HLD

`[BP]`
- **Post service** → writes the post (7.3) → emits a "new post" event (Part 9).
- **Fan-out service** (async worker consuming the event — Part 9): for a normal author, **push** postId into each active follower's feed (cache — Part 6); for a **celebrity** (follower count above a threshold), **skip push** (mark for pull).
- **Feed service (read):** fetch the user's **precomputed feed** (cache — Part 6), **merge in followed celebrities' recent posts** (pull), **rank** (§3.6), paginate (cursor), return.
- **Feed store:** per-user feed lists in a fast store/cache (Redis — 6.6), capped length (only recent N — 6.4), rebuildable from posts + follows.

### 3.6 Deep dives + bottlenecks

`[BP]`
- **Celebrity/hot-key** (7.4): the make-or-break — **hybrid push/pull** avoids the 100M-write fan-out; celebrity posts merged at read (§3.4). (Same skew lesson as 7.4/18.6.)
- **Write amplification** (§3.2): push trades read cost for write cost; hybrid + async fan-out (Part 9) + skipping inactive users bounds it.
- **Ranking:** chronological (simplest) → **ML-ranked** (engagement-predicting model — hooks into **19.2.7** recommendations); rank at read over the merged candidate set. Keep candidate generation (fan-out) separate from ranking (18.5-style online/offline split).
- **Pagination:** **cursor-based** (opaque cursor = position/timestamp+id), not offset — stable under a feed that's constantly growing (offset would skip/duplicate — a classic mistake).
- **Consistency:** **eventual** (10.5) — a post may take seconds to appear in all feeds (async fan-out); acceptable. Read-your-writes for your **own** posts (show them immediately — 10.3).
- **Feed storage/caching:** feeds are **cached, capped, rebuildable** derived data (Part 6) — losing them just means recompute; source of truth is posts + follows.
- **Bottleneck:** the **write-side fan-out** (celebrity amplification — 7.4) → hybrid + async workers + caps. Reads are cheap (precomputed + cache).
- `[BP]` **The lesson:** news feed = **hybrid fan-out (push normal / pull celebrities — 7.4) + async fan-out workers (Part 9) + cached precomputed feeds (Part 6) + read-time merge/rank + cursor pagination**. The whole design bends around the **follower-graph skew**.

## 5. Real-World Analogy

Think of **delivering a newsletter to subscribers, where most authors have a few subscribers but a few superstars have millions**.

- **Push (fan-out-on-write) = pre-stuffing every subscriber's mailbox** the moment an author publishes. Reading your mail is instant (it's already there), but if a **superstar with 100 million subscribers** publishes, you'd have to stuff 100 million mailboxes at once — impossible in real time.
- **Pull (fan-out-on-read) = assembling your newsletter only when you check the mail** — the courier runs to every author you follow and gathers their latest. One publish is cheap, but assembling on every read is slow.
- **Hybrid = pre-stuff for normal authors, fetch-on-read for superstars.** Your mailbox is mostly pre-filled (fast), and when you open it, the courier just grabs the handful of superstars you follow and slots their latest in. Cheap reads, no impossible superstar stuffing.
- **Ranking = the editor arranging the newsletter** by what you'll care about most, not strictly by arrival time.
- **Cursor pagination = a bookmark, not a page number:** since new items keep arriving at the top, "give me page 2" (offset) would skip or repeat items; a bookmark ("continue after this exact item") stays stable.

## 7. Implementation Details

- **Hybrid fan-out** (push normal / pull celebrities by follower-count threshold — 7.4) via **async workers** (Part 9) (§3.4/3.5).
- **Precomputed feeds** in Redis (6.6), capped to recent N (6.4), rebuildable from posts+follows (§3.5).
- **Read path:** fetch precomputed feed + merge followed celebrities' recent posts + rank + cursor-paginate (§3.5/3.6).
- **Ranking:** chronological → ML (separate candidate generation from ranking — 18.5/19.2.7).
- **Eventual consistency** (10.5) with read-your-writes for own posts (10.3).
- **Cursor pagination** (not offset) for stability (§3.6).

## 8–14. (Condensed)

**Advantages:** cheap fast reads (precomputed), no celebrity write explosion (hybrid), decoupled async fan-out, ranking pluggable.
**Disadvantages/cautions:** eventual consistency (posts appear with lag); feed storage cost (precomputed lists); hybrid adds read-path complexity (merge); ranking needs a separate ML system (19.2.7).
**When NOT to use push:** for celebrities / low-active-follower ratios — pull or hybrid instead (7.4).
**Common mistakes:** pure push (celebrity write explosion); pure pull (slow reads on a read-heavy path); offset pagination (skips/dupes on a growing feed); synchronous fan-out (blocks the post request); unbounded feed lists.
**Interview Qs:** 🟢 Read-heavy or write-heavy, and why? 🟡 Push vs pull vs hybrid — tradeoffs? 🔴 How do you solve the celebrity problem? Ranking + pagination? ⚫ Full design: hybrid fan-out, async workers, cached feeds, read-time merge+rank, cursor pagination, eventual consistency, and the write-amplification bottleneck.
**Production pitfalls:** celebrity hot-keys (7.4); fan-out backlog under viral events (queue lag — 9.9); feed-cache stampede (6.7); inconsistent ranking causing feed "jumping."
**Optimizations:** skip inactive followers on push; cap + expire feeds (6.4); batch fan-out writes; separate candidate-generation from ranking; edge/CDN for media (18.4); precompute celebrity recent-posts cache.

## 15. Summary

A **news feed** (Twitter/Facebook/Instagram) is the archetypal **massive-read-fan-out + write-amplification** design; this deep treatment extends **19.1.5**, centering on the **fan-out strategy** and the **celebrity/hot-key problem (7.4)**. It's **read-heavy** (feed loads ≫ posts, ~100:1+), **latency-sensitive** on reads, and **eventually consistent** is fine (a post appearing seconds late is acceptable — 10.5). The **core decision** is how a post reaches followers: **fan-out-on-write (push)** inserts the postId into every follower's **precomputed feed** immediately (cheap reads, but **write amplification** — a celebrity's post = millions of writes — 7.4); **fan-out-on-read (pull)** stores only own posts and **merges followees' posts at read time** (cheap writes, expensive reads — bad for the read-heavy path); the **hybrid** (the senior answer) **pushes for normal users** but **pulls for celebrities** — at read time it merges the few followed celebrities' recent posts on top of the pushed feed, getting cheap reads with no celebrity write explosion. The **HLD**: a post emits an event (Part 9); an **async fan-out worker** pushes to active followers' feed caches (Redis — 6.6, capped/rebuildable — 6.4) but **skips celebrities**; the **read path** fetches the precomputed feed, merges followed-celebrity posts, **ranks** (chronological → ML — hooks into **19.2.7**, keeping candidate-generation separate from ranking à la 18.5), and **cursor-paginates** (opaque cursor, **not offset** — stable over a growing feed). **Deep dives:** the celebrity hot-key (hybrid — 7.4), write amplification (async + caps), ranking (ML), pagination (cursor), consistency (eventual + read-your-writes for own posts — 10.3), and feed storage (cached, capped, rebuildable derived data — Part 6). The **bottleneck is the write-side fan-out** (celebrity amplification), dissolved by hybrid + async workers + caps; reads stay cheap (precomputed + cached). The whole design **bends around the follower-graph skew**.

## 16. Revision Notes (flashcard-ready)

- **Q:** Read-heavy or write-heavy? **A:** Read-heavy (feed loads ≫ posts) — but write-side fan-out amplification is the hard part.
- **Q:** Push (fan-out-on-write)? **A:** Insert postId into every follower's precomputed feed on post → cheap reads, write amplification (bad for celebrities).
- **Q:** Pull (fan-out-on-read)? **A:** Merge followees' posts at read time → cheap writes, expensive reads (bad for read-heavy path).
- **Q:** Hybrid (the answer)? **A:** Push for normal users, pull for celebrities (merge their recent posts at read) — cheap reads, no celebrity write explosion (7.4).
- **Q:** Celebrity problem? **A:** A post to 100M followers = 100M writes if pushed → skip push, pull at read.
- **Q:** Fan-out mechanism? **A:** Async workers consuming post events (Part 9); precomputed feeds cached (Redis), capped, rebuildable.
- **Q:** Pagination? **A:** Cursor-based (opaque position), not offset — stable over a growing feed.
- **Q:** Consistency? **A:** Eventual (posts appear with lag); read-your-writes for your own posts (10.3).
- **Q:** Ranking? **A:** Chronological → ML-ranked; separate candidate generation (fan-out) from ranking (19.2.7/18.5).
