## 1. Learning Objectives

After this lesson you will be able to:

- Lay out the **consistency spectrum** — from **strong (linearizable)** through **sequential**, **causal**, session guarantees, to **eventual** — and explain what each promises about *what readers see*.
- Connect consistency strength to **cost**: stronger = more **coordination** (consensus — 8.3), **higher latency**, and **lower availability** (CAP — 10.7); weaker = cheaper, more available, but more anomalies (10.3).
- Explain **causal consistency** as the "sweet spot" (preserves happens-before — 8.2.3 — without consensus → highly available) and **eventual consistency** as the weakest useful guarantee (converge if writes stop).
- Choose the **weakest consistency that's correct** per data type — the master skill — and connect the spectrum to ordering (8.2.3), conflicts (10.4), and CAP/PACELC (10.7/10.8).

## 2. Motivation — "Consistency" is not one thing; it's a dial

"Is your system consistent?" is a meaningless question until you say **which** consistency, because **consistency is a spectrum**, not a binary. This lesson organizes the models you've met piecemeal — total vs partial order (8.2.3), session guarantees (10.3), eventual consistency (10.1/10.4), linearizability (8.2.4/8.3) — into **one ordered spectrum** from **strongest (linearizable/strong)** to **weakest useful (eventual)**, with **causal** and **session** guarantees in between. This matters enormously because **consistency strength directly trades against cost, latency, and availability**: the stronger the guarantee, the more **coordination** it requires (up to full **consensus** — 8.3), the **higher the latency**, and the **lower the availability under partition** (CAP — 10.7). There is no free lunch — you can't have the strongest consistency *and* the highest availability *and* the lowest latency.

So the master skill is **choosing the weakest consistency model that is still correct for each piece of data** (echoing 8.2.3's "weakest correct order" and 1.1.5's tradeoffs). A bank balance may need **strong (linearizable)** consistency (any staleness is a correctness bug); a social feed is fine with **eventual** or **causal** consistency (a slightly-stale feed is acceptable, and users just shouldn't see effects before causes). Over-choosing strong consistency everywhere wastes availability and latency (and may be impossible under partition); under-choosing produces correctness bugs. Between the extremes sits **causal consistency** — the "sweet spot" that preserves the **happens-before** order (8.2.3 — effects never precede causes) and combines the session guarantees of 10.3, all **without requiring consensus**, so it stays **highly available** — enough to prevent the jarring anomalies while remaining cheap. This lesson defines each model precisely, orders them by strength and cost, and gives the decision framework — the conceptual center of Part 10 that CAP (10.7) and linearizability-vs-serializability (10.6) then sharpen.

## 3. Theory — From first principles

### 3.1 What "consistency" means here

`[CS]` A **consistency model** is a **contract about what values reads can return** given the history of writes — i.e., **how up-to-date and how ordered** the data a reader sees is guaranteed to be, across replicas (10.1) `[CS]`. (Note: this is **replication/distributed consistency** — "the C in CAP" — *distinct* from the **C in ACID** — transactional invariants — 5.2.1. This lesson is about the former: what replicas show readers.) Stronger models make **more promises** (readers see a more up-to-date, more-ordered, more-agreed view) at **more cost** (coordination/latency/availability); weaker models make **fewer promises** (readers may see stale/out-of-order/divergent data) but are **cheaper and more available**.

### 3.2 The spectrum (strongest → weakest)

`[CS]` The main models, from strongest to weakest:

1. **Strong consistency / Linearizability (10.6):** the system behaves as if there's **one single copy** of the data and every operation takes effect **atomically at a single point in time**, consistent with **real-time order** (if op A completes before op B starts, everyone sees A before B). Reads always return the **most recent** write. **The strongest, most intuitive** (behaves like one machine) — but requires **coordination/consensus** (8.3), giving **highest latency** and **lowest availability** (must block under partition — CAP — 10.7). (Detailed in 10.6.)

2. **Sequential consistency:** all operations appear in **some single total order** that all nodes agree on, and each client's operations appear **in its program order** — but **not necessarily consistent with real-time** (a write may not be immediately visible; the global order needn't match wall-clock order). Weaker than linearizable (drops the real-time requirement), still a **single agreed total order** (needs coordination). (Historically important; linearizability is the more-used strong model in practice.)

3. **Causal consistency:** preserves the **happens-before (causal) order** (8.2.3/8.2.2) — everyone sees **causally-related** operations in **causal order** (effects never before causes), but **concurrent** operations **may be seen in different orders** by different nodes. **The sweet spot** (§3.3): achievable **without consensus** (via version vectors / causal broadcast — 8.2.2), so **highly available** and low-latency, while preventing the jarring anomalies (reply-before-question — 10.3). Encompasses the **session guarantees** (10.3 — read-your-writes, monotonic reads, consistent prefix all follow from causal consistency).

4. **Session / client-centric guarantees (10.3):** **read-your-writes, monotonic reads, consistent prefix** — per-client promises that make **one client's experience sensible**, between causal and eventual (often grouped with causal). (Covered in 10.3.)

5. **Eventual consistency:** the weakest useful guarantee — **if writes stop, all replicas eventually converge** to the same value. **No promise** about what readers see in the meantime (stale, out-of-order, divergent). **Cheapest, most available** (no coordination — leaderless/multi-leader defaults — 10.1), but all the anomalies (10.3) and conflicts (10.4) are possible. "Eventually, everyone agrees" — with no bound on "eventually" (though practically it's fast).

### 3.3 Causal consistency — the sweet spot

`[CS]`/`[EMERGING]` **Causal consistency** deserves emphasis as the pragmatic middle ground `[OPINION]`:
- It **preserves causality** (happens-before — 8.2.3): if write B **causally depends on** write A (a reply to a message, an edit after a create), **everyone sees A before B** — so **no effect appears before its cause** (fixing the consistent-prefix anomaly — 10.3). Concurrent (unrelated) writes may be seen in different orders — which is **fine** (they're genuinely unordered — 8.2.3).
- **Crucially, it needs NO consensus** — it's achievable with **version vectors / causal broadcast** (8.2.2), so it's **highly available** (survives partitions — the strongest consistency that CAP-allows during a partition — 10.7) and **low-latency** (no coordination round-trips).
- It **subsumes the session guarantees** (10.3) — read-your-writes, monotonic reads, and consistent prefix all hold under causal consistency.
- **Why it's the sweet spot:** it eliminates the **anomalies that actually confuse users** (effect-before-cause, your-own-writes) **without** paying strong consistency's coordination/availability cost. For many systems (social, collaborative, messaging), **causal is exactly enough** — stronger is wasteful, weaker is buggy.

### 3.4 The strength ↔ cost tradeoff

`[CS]` The spectrum is fundamentally a **cost/availability curve**:
- **Stronger (linearizable/sequential):** requires **coordination/consensus** (8.3 — total order = consensus, 8.2.3) → **higher latency** (round-trips to agree), **lower availability** (must **block** under partition to avoid divergence — CAP chooses C over A — 10.7), lower throughput (coordination is the USL wall — 7.1).
- **Weaker (causal/eventual):** **less/no coordination** → **lower latency**, **higher availability** (stays up under partition — CAP chooses A over C), higher throughput — but **more anomalies** (stale/out-of-order reads — 10.3) and **conflicts** (10.4).
- **The knee:** **causal consistency** sits at a sweet spot — **no consensus** (so available/fast like eventual) but **preserves causality** (so anomaly-free like stronger models for causally-related data). Below it (eventual) you lose ordering; above it (linearizable) you pay consensus.
This is why "choose the weakest correct consistency" is the master skill — you're **buying only the coordination you actually need.**

### 3.5 Choosing the weakest correct consistency

`[BP]` The decision framework (echoing 8.2.3, 1.1.5):
| Data / need | Consistency |
|---|---|
| Money, inventory-at-checkout, uniqueness, "read the latest" mandatory | **Strong (linearizable)** — accept coordination/latency/availability cost |
| A single agreed global order (ledger sequence, leader) | **Strong / sequential** (consensus) |
| Social feeds, messaging, collaborative, "no effect-before-cause but staleness OK" | **Causal** (sweet spot — no consensus, available) |
| Per-user sensible experience (own writes, no regression) | **Session guarantees** (≈ causal) — 10.3 |
| Analytics, metrics, caches, "converge eventually is fine" | **Eventual** (cheapest, most available) |

- **Default to the weakest that's still correct.** Ask: "what's the *worst* a reader could see, and is that acceptable?" If stale/out-of-order is a **correctness bug** (money) → strong; if it's just a **minor UX blemish** (feed) → causal/eventual.
- **Mix within a system** — a bank uses **strong** for balances, **eventual** for the activity feed, **causal** for messages. Consistency is chosen **per data type**, not globally.
- **Confine strong consistency to the small core that needs it** (8.2.3 §3.6, 8.3) — the ledger/uniqueness — and run the bulk causally/eventually for availability and scale.

### 3.6 Relationship to ordering, conflicts, CAP

`[CS]` The spectrum ties together everything in Parts 8 and 10:
- **Ordering (8.2.3):** consistency strength ≈ ordering strength. **Eventual** ≈ no ordering guarantee; **causal** ≈ partial (happens-before) order; **sequential/linearizable** ≈ total order. **Total order = consensus** (8.2.3), which is *why* strong consistency needs coordination.
- **Conflicts (10.4):** weaker consistency (write-anywhere) → conflicts to resolve (LWW/CRDT/siblings); strong consistency (single-orderer/consensus) → no conflicts. CRDTs give **strong *eventual* consistency** (10.4) — convergence without coordination, but not linearizability.
- **CAP/PACELC (10.7/10.8):** the spectrum is the **consistency** axis of these theorems — under partition, you trade consistency for availability; the model you can offer during a partition is **at most causal** (linearizable requires blocking — CP). PACELC adds the latency-vs-consistency tradeoff in normal operation.
So the consistency spectrum is the **unifying frame**: ordering, conflicts, and CAP/PACELC are all facets of "how strong a consistency guarantee, at what coordination cost."

### 3.7 A note on terminology and precision

`[BP]` Consistency terminology is notoriously muddled (marketing muddies it further):
- **"Strong consistency"** usually means **linearizability** (10.6) in distributed-systems contexts.
- **Consistency (CAP/replication)** ≠ **Consistency (ACID)** — CAP-C is about replicas showing readers a consistent view; ACID-C is transactional invariants (5.2.1). **Linearizability ≠ serializability** (10.6 — they're orthogonal: one about single-object real-time order, the other about transaction isolation).
- **Vendors' "consistency levels"** (e.g., Cassandra's ONE/QUORUM, Cosmos DB's five levels) map onto this spectrum — read the fine print to know **which** guarantee you're getting.
Be **precise**: name the exact model (linearizable? causal? eventual? read-your-writes?), don't say "consistent"/"strongly consistent" loosely. 10.6 sharpens the most-confused pair (linearizability vs serializability).

## 5. Real-World Analogy

Imagine keeping a group informed via a **shared whiteboard** that's copied to several rooms, and asking "how in-sync must the copies be?"

- **Eventual consistency:** the copies are updated **whenever someone gets around to it** — for a while, different rooms show **different things**, but if updates stop, they **all eventually match**. Cheap and never blocks anyone, but you might read a **stale** board or see updates in a weird order. Fine for a **"lunch menu"** board (a little staleness is harmless).
- **Causal consistency (the sweet spot):** the rooms may still differ on **unrelated** items, but **cause-and-effect is always respected** — you'll **never** see the answer "Yes!" before the question "Should we launch?" (effects never precede causes). Nobody has to **stop and coordinate** to guarantee this — it's cheap and always available — yet it eliminates the **nonsensical** experiences. For a **discussion board**, this is exactly enough.
- **Strong consistency (linearizable):** every room shows the **exact same, fully-up-to-date board at every instant** — as if there's **one physical whiteboard** everyone looks at. Wonderfully simple to reason about, but to guarantee it, whenever someone writes, **all rooms must coordinate** before the write "counts" (slow), and if a room gets **cut off** (partition), you must either **stop accepting writes** (unavailable) or break the guarantee. Necessary for a **"current account balance"** board (any staleness = a real error), but overkill and costly for a menu.
- **The master skill:** you don't make **every** board strongly consistent (expensive, fragile) or **every** board eventually consistent (buggy for money). You **match the guarantee to the board**: the **balance** board is strongly consistent (coordinate, accept the cost), the **discussion** board is causally consistent (respect cause/effect, stay available), and the **menu** board is eventually consistent (cheapest). And you keep the expensive strong-consistency machinery for the **few boards that truly need it.**

## 7. Implementation Details — choosing and applying

- **Name the exact model you need** per data type (linearizable? causal? read-your-writes? eventual?) — don't say "consistent" loosely (§3.7) `[BP]`.
- **Default to the weakest that's still correct** (§3.5): ask "is stale/out-of-order a correctness bug or a minor blemish?" — bug → strong; blemish → causal/eventual.
- **Use strong (linearizable)** only where mandatory (money, uniqueness, inventory-at-checkout, leader/config) — via **consensus/single-leader reads** (8.3), accepting latency/availability cost (§3.2/3.5).
- **Use causal consistency** (the sweet spot) for feeds/messaging/collaborative — preserves happens-before **without consensus** → available + anomaly-free (via version vectors/causal broadcast — 8.2.2) (§3.3).
- **Use session guarantees** (10.3) for per-user sensible experience (read-your-writes, monotonic) — cheap, ≈ causal.
- **Use eventual** for analytics/metrics/caches where convergence suffices — cheapest, most available (§3.2).
- **Confine strong consistency to a small core** (ledger/uniqueness) and run the bulk causally/eventually for scale/availability (§3.5, 8.2.3).
- **Understand the CAP/PACELC implication** (10.7/10.8) — strong = must block under partition (CP); causal/eventual = stay available (AP) (§3.6).
- **Read vendor "consistency levels" precisely** — map them to this spectrum (§3.7).

## 8. Advantages (by choosing well)

- **Strong:** simplest to reason about (behaves like one copy); correctness for invariant/latest-required data (§3.2).
- **Causal:** anomaly-free (no effect-before-cause) **without** consensus → available + low-latency (the sweet spot) (§3.3).
- **Session:** per-user sensible experience, cheap (10.3).
- **Eventual:** cheapest, most available, highest throughput; fine for convergence-tolerant data (§3.2).
- **Choosing the weakest correct model:** buys only the coordination you need → best availability/latency/cost for the requirement (§3.4/3.5).

---

## 9. Disadvantages / costs

- **Strong:** **coordination/consensus cost** → high latency, low availability (blocks under partition — CAP), lower throughput (§3.2/3.4).
- **Causal:** needs causal-tracking machinery (version vectors — 8.2.2); doesn't give a single agreed order (can't enforce global invariants — 10.4) (§3.3).
- **Eventual:** all the anomalies (10.3) and conflicts (10.4); no ordering/freshness promise (§3.2).
- **Terminology confusion** — muddled terms; CAP-C ≠ ACID-C; linearizable ≠ serializable (§3.7, 10.6).
- **Mismatched choice:** too strong = wasted availability/latency; too weak = correctness bugs (§3.5).

---

## 10. When NOT to / limits

- **Don't use strong consistency everywhere** — wastes availability/latency and may be impossible under partition; confine it to the data that needs it (§3.5, 10.7).
- **Don't use eventual consistency for correctness-critical data** (money, uniqueness) — you'll get correctness bugs (§3.5).
- **Don't assume causal consistency enforces global invariants** — it doesn't (no single agreed order); use strong/consensus for those (§3.3/3.6, 10.4).
- **Don't say "strongly consistent" loosely** — name the exact model; know linearizable ≠ serializable (§3.7, 10.6).
- **Don't ignore the CAP implication** — strong consistency can't be highly available under partition (10.7).

---

## 11. Common Mistakes

1. **Treating consistency as binary** ("consistent or not") instead of a spectrum → wrong model choice (§3.1/3.2).
2. **Strong consistency everywhere** → needless latency/availability loss; may be impossible under partition (§3.5, 10.7).
3. **Eventual consistency for money/uniqueness** → correctness bugs (stale reads, violated invariants) (§3.5, 10.4).
4. **Assuming causal/eventual can enforce invariants** → uniqueness/balance violations (need consensus) (§3.6, 10.4).
5. **Confusing CAP-C with ACID-C, or linearizable with serializable** → reasoning errors (§3.7, 10.6).
6. **Not confining strong consistency to a small core** → the whole system pays consensus cost (§3.5, 8.2.3).
7. **Trusting a vendor's "strong"/"consistent" label** without checking the exact guarantee (§3.7).

## 13. Production Pitfalls

- **Strong-everywhere latency/availability hit:** using linearizable consistency for data that didn't need it → slow writes, outages under partition (§3.5, 10.7).
- **Eventual-for-money bug:** a balance/inventory read from an eventually-consistent store → user acts on stale data / oversell / overdraw (§3.5, 10.4).
- **Invariant violated under eventual/causal:** two "unique" usernames or a negative balance because no single agreed order enforced the invariant (§3.6, 10.4) — needed consensus.
- **Anomalies from choosing eventual over causal:** effect-before-cause (reply before question) where causal consistency would have prevented it (§3.3, 10.3).
- **Terminology-driven mistake:** assuming "strongly consistent" (vendor label) meant linearizable + serializable when it meant something weaker (§3.7, 10.6).
- **Whole-system consensus bottleneck:** not confining strong consistency to a core → coordination limits system-wide throughput/availability (§3.5, 7.1).

## 14. Optimization Techniques

- **Choose the weakest correct model per data type** — buy only needed coordination (§3.4/3.5) `[BP]`.
- **Causal consistency** for anomaly-free-but-available data (version vectors/causal broadcast — 8.2.2) — the sweet spot (§3.3).
- **Session guarantees** (10.3) for cheap per-user correctness.
- **Confine strong consistency/consensus to a small core** (ledger/uniqueness/leader) — bulk runs causal/eventual (§3.5, 8.2.3, 8.3.8).
- **Tunable consistency levels** (per-request) where the store supports it (Cassandra/Cosmos) to match each operation (§3.7).
- **CRDTs** for strong *eventual* consistency (convergence without coordination) where invariants aren't required (10.4).
- **Be precise about the model** and its CAP/PACELC implications (§3.6/3.7, 10.7/10.8).

## 15. Summary

**Consistency is a spectrum, not a binary** — a set of contracts about **what values reads can return** given the write history across replicas (the "C in CAP," distinct from ACID-C — 5.2.1). From **strongest to weakest**: **strong/linearizable** (behaves like a **single copy**; every operation atomic at a point in time, consistent with **real-time order**; reads return the latest — the most intuitive but requires **consensus/coordination** → highest latency, lowest availability, must block under partition — 10.6/10.7); **sequential** (a single agreed **total order** in program order, but **not** real-time — still needs coordination); **causal** (preserves the **happens-before/partial order** — 8.2.3 — so **effects never precede causes**, while **concurrent** operations may be seen in different orders — achievable **without consensus** via version vectors/causal broadcast — 8.2.2 — so **highly available and low-latency**, and it **subsumes the session guarantees** of 10.3 — the **sweet spot**); **session guarantees** (read-your-writes, monotonic reads, consistent prefix — per-client sensible experience — 10.3); and **eventual** (the weakest useful: **if writes stop, replicas eventually converge**, with **no promise** about the interim — cheapest, most available, but all anomalies — 10.3 — and conflicts — 10.4). The spectrum is fundamentally a **strength ↔ cost/availability curve**: **stronger = more coordination (up to consensus — 8.3) = higher latency + lower availability** (CAP chooses C over A under partition — 10.7); **weaker = less coordination = faster + more available** but more anomalies. **Causal consistency sits at the knee** — no consensus (available/fast like eventual) yet preserves causality (anomaly-free for causally-related data) — and is the **strongest model achievable while staying available under partition** (10.7). The **master skill** is **choosing the weakest consistency that's still correct per data type** (8.2.3, 1.1.5): if stale/out-of-order is a **correctness bug** (money, uniqueness, inventory) → **strong**; if it's a **minor UX blemish** (feeds, messaging) → **causal/session**; if convergence suffices (analytics, caches) → **eventual** — **mixing within a system** (strong balances + eventual feeds + causal messages) and **confining strong consistency/consensus to the small core** that needs it (ledger/uniqueness/leader) while the bulk runs causally/eventually for scale and availability. The spectrum **unifies** ordering (8.2.3 — eventual≈none, causal≈partial, sequential/linearizable≈total, and total-order=consensus), conflicts (10.4 — weaker → conflicts to resolve; CRDTs give strong *eventual* consistency), and **CAP/PACELC** (10.7/10.8 — the consistency axis). Be **precise** about which model you mean (linearizable ≠ serializable — 10.6; CAP-C ≠ ACID-C; vendor "consistency levels" map onto this spectrum) — over-choosing strong wastes availability/latency, under-choosing produces correctness bugs.
